# Case — Coding to Improve Executive Functioning in Children with Sickle Cell Disease

Multiple-baseline single-case feasibility study. **Journal of Intelligence, 2026.** Closest published analog to our pilot population (chronically ill, cognitively affected children, code.org as vehicle).

## Design
- **Participants**: 3 children, **ages 7–8**, sickle cell disease.
- **Cognitive profile**: lower-range IQ (71–82), executive function (EF) impairments.
- **Dose**: **11 coding sessions over 5–6 weeks**. Platform: **code.org**.
- **Measures**: pre/post non-verbal EF (planning, inhibition, switching) + verbal EF (working memory, phonological/semantic fluency).

## Outcomes
- **100% adherence** to intervention.
- Significant coding gains (IRD range 0.69–0.79 — "large effect" territory for single-case).
- **Transfer effects**: non-verbal planning z-score gains >2; verbal fluency z-score gains 0.47–1.04.
- Inter-individual variability tied to baseline cognitive profile.

## What applies to HypeProof pilot
- code.org-style **playful problem-solving** is feasible and well-tolerated by chronically ill kids with EF deficits → supports [[no-debug-philosophy]].
- 100% adherence signals **intrinsic engagement** possible even with cognitively impaired children — our [[ai-persona-workflows]] can leverage this.
- Age 7–8 lower bound matches our **8–12세** target well.

## Limits / What does NOT transfer
- Study was **11 sessions**, not single-shot. Our 90-min one-off cannot claim EF transfer — set expectations on **experiential delight**, not measurable cognitive gain.
- N=3. Single-case methodology.
- Sickle cell ≠ pediatric oncology (different cognitive mechanism, different immunosuppression profile).

> [!contradiction] Multi-session EF transfer ≠ single-session outcome. Use this paper to justify feasibility and engagement, **not** to promise cognitive benefit from our pilot.
